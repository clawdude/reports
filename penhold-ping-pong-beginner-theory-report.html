Beginner Penhold Ping Pong Theory Guide

How to start with the penhold grip: what the grip is for, how to hold the racket, how to stand, how to serve, and what to practise first.

Prepared: 3 May 2026 Topic: penhold grip for beginners Scope: theory-first, image-linked, no generated images

Executive summary

Best beginner path: learn a relaxed Chinese penhold grip, build a reliable forehand-first game, add a simple traditional backhand block/push immediately, then gradually learn reverse penhold backhand (RPB) so the backhand side does not become a permanent weakness.

Why penhold?

Penhold gives excellent wrist freedom, spin variation, short-game touch, and serve disguise. It naturally supports a forehand-dominant style.

Main beginner risk

The backhand can lag badly. If you only run around every backhand, you will develop footwork stress and predictable patterns.

What to practise first

Grip pressure, ready stance, forehand counterhit, backhand block/push, legal short backspin serve, then simple sidespin/pendulum serve ideas.

1. What “penhold” means

The penhold family is named for holding the racket somewhat like a pen: thumb and index finger wrap around the front of the handle while the remaining fingers sit on the back. The ITTF rules do not require one specific grip; the grip is a style choice rather than a rule requirement.

There are two beginner-relevant variants:

VariantWhat it meansBeginner recommendation
Traditional penholdMostly uses one rubber face. Backhand is usually a block/push/punch with the forehand face turned across the body.Learn the basic block/push, but do not rely on it forever.
Modern Chinese penholdUses the back rubber for reverse penhold backhand (RPB), enabling more attacking backhand strokes.Best long-term path for a beginner who wants penhold in the modern game.

2. How to hold the racket

  1. Front side: wrap the thumb and index finger around the blade/handle as if holding a thick pen.
  2. Back side: curl the middle, ring, and little fingers lightly on the back of the blade. Do not flatten them so much that they block the RPB contact zone.
  3. Pressure: hold firm enough that the racket does not wobble, but loose enough that the wrist can move. A death grip kills spin and touch.
  4. Face angle: keep the racket face slightly closed for topspin/counterhit, more open for push/backspin, and adjustable for serves.
Common mistake: beginners often squeeze with the thumb and index finger until the wrist locks. Penhold’s advantage is wrist freedom; if the wrist is frozen, you lose the point of the grip.

3. Stance and body position

For a beginner penholder, stance matters even more than grip detail because the style is forehand-friendly. You need to move your body so the forehand can cover more balls without reaching.

  • Feet: slightly wider than shoulder width.
  • Knees: bent, not locked.
  • Hips/torso: hinge slightly forward from hips, not just from the neck.
  • Weight: balanced toward the balls of the feet, ready to push left/right.
  • Racket: in front of the body, not hanging by the hip.

PingSkills’ stance discussion emphasizes the balance problem beginners have when they bend only the knees or only the upper body. The useful cue is: lower the body by bending both knees and hips, then find a stable middle point where you can be pushed/pulled lightly without falling.

4. Stroke theory for beginners

Forehand counterhit

This is your first “normal rally” stroke. Rotate the waist slightly, keep the elbow relaxed, contact the ball in front/right of the body, and finish forward-up. The penhold forehand should feel connected from feet → hips → forearm → wrist, not like a wrist-only slap.

Backhand block/push

Even if your long-term goal is RPB, immediately learn a simple backhand block and push. This prevents the classic beginner penhold problem: every ball to the backhand becomes panic footwork.

Reverse penhold backhand (RPB)

RPB uses the back side of the racket for backhand attacks. Keep the curled fingers out of the hitting zone, relax the index finger, and let the thumb help rotate the forehand face inward. Start with slow contact and placement before trying power.

5. Serving theory

Serving is where penhold’s wrist mobility is especially useful. But a beginner should first make serves legal, low, and repeatable before chasing heavy disguise.

Legal serve checklist

  • Start with the ball resting freely on the open palm.
  • Toss the ball upward; do not hide it with body or arm.
  • Contact the ball behind the end line and above the table surface.
  • In singles, the ball must bounce once on your side, then once on the opponent’s side.

First serve: short backspin/chop serve

This is the safest first serve because it teaches thin brushing contact and keeps the return from being easily attacked. Brush under the ball, keep the first bounce near your side of the table, and aim for a second bounce near the opponent’s net area.

Second serve: simple pendulum serve

Once you can serve short and legal, add a pendulum motion for sidespin. The goal is not maximum spin yet; the goal is learning how racket angle and brushing direction change the receiver’s return.

6. Beginner practice plan

PhaseFocusPass condition
Week 1Grip, stance, forehand counterhit, simple backhand blockYou can rally 20 slow balls without changing grip every shot.
Week 2Short push, backhand push/block, footwork from backhand corner to forehandYou can return slow backspin without popping every ball high.
Week 3Legal short backspin serve and basic receive8/10 serves are legal, low, and land short or half-long.
Week 4Intro RPB and simple pendulum serveYou can make controlled RPB contact and vary serve spin direction intentionally.
Simple daily drill: 5 minutes grip/stance shadow swings, 10 minutes forehand counterhit, 10 minutes backhand block/push, 10 minutes serving to targets. Quality beats volume.

7. What to avoid

  • Do not choose Japanese penhold only because it looks cool. It can produce a great forehand, but the backhand burden is high for beginners.
  • Do not ignore the backhand. Even forehand-dominant penholders need a safe backhand answer.
  • Do not copy elite serve motion before legal basics. Advanced serves hide subtle spin changes; beginners need repeatable contact first.
  • Do not grip too tight. Tight hand = stiff wrist = weak spin and poor touch.
